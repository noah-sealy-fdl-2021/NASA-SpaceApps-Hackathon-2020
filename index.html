Gas Emission vs Chlorophyll Concentration

by Zarreen Naowal Reza and Noah Sealy for NASA SpaceApps Hackathon, October 2020

Our Mission

Phytoplankton are a key nessecity in the health of oceans, which contribute to a large portion of the World's biodiversity.
NASA claims Phytoplankton to have a huge role in the food chains of many living creatures in bodies of water.
A method used to record Phytoplankton is to record chlorophyll levels in the sea, as Phytoplankton needs chlorophyll to properly survive.
Chlorophyll can be recorded through colour imagery, as its presence is indicated by the color green in bodies of water.
"Chlorophyll, which absorbs red light (resulting in the ocean's blue-green colour) is considered a good indicator of the health of the ocean and its level of productivity."
Rising gas emission levels from everday human processes are a growing constant which threatens the wellbeing of the World's climate, and the biodivesity which inhabits it.
This biodiversity includes the inhabitants of the oceans the World holds. As climate change becomes more and more prevalent, data science researchers are able to use various data to show its growing side effects.
For the October 2020 NASA Space Apps Hackathon, we wanted to take on a type of challenge that a data scientist may have in mind.
The question we thought we would be able to answer with avilable dataset was based on the discussed information.
Will the chlorophyll concentration in specific bodies of water reduce with the evergrowing amount of gases such as CO2 and NO increase in the atmosphere?
In order to answer this question we pulled gas emissions data from the SCISAT dataset, and ocean chlorophyll concentration data from the OceanColor dataset, and attempted to examine the data using models from the field of data science.
